# 2 Weeks Ready — Build Kits

Plan to be on your own for at least 2 weeks. After a major disaster, stores may be closed and emergency services may take days or weeks to reach you. Every household should assemble an emergency supply kit with enough provisions to last two weeks per person and per pet.

Recommended items for a 2-week emergency kit:

- Water — 1 gallon per person, per day
- Food — non-perishable, two-week supply
- Comfort and entertainment items
- Medical equipment
- Glasses and eye care
- First aid kit
- Can opener (manual)
- Sturdy shoes
- NOAA alert radio
- Extra batteries
- Flashlight
- Warm clothes
- Personal hygiene items
- Fire extinguisher
- Tools
- Pet supplies
- Cash
- Toilet paper
- Medications
- Identification and important documents

Keep at least 2 weeks of supplies in your home. Have smaller kits for work, for every family member, and pets. Have a vehicle safety kit too.

## Be Prepared — Make a Plan

Determine an accessible meeting place in case you have to leave your home or work, so you can reunite your family. Learn about disaster plans for: your work, your children's school, your medical and/or transportation provider, and other places where your family spends time.

Check with your local emergency management office to:
- find out what hazards could affect your home and place of work
- sign up for emergency alerts and notifications
- identify your local Emergency Alert System (EAS) radio stations
- know your evacuation routes and alternate transportation routes

## Develop a Communications Plan

Write down emergency contact information for every member of your household and keep a copy in each go-bag. Texts are more likely to go through than voice calls when networks are congested after a disaster, so plan to communicate by SMS first.

Have an extra cell phone charger and spare batteries in your kit. Choose an out-of-area contact who can serve as a relay point for the family — when local lines are jammed, long-distance calls often still connect.

## Help Each Other

Know how you'll help people and pets who normally rely on you. Help organizations in your neighborhood that may need support during a disaster.

Attend free disaster skills workshops offered by your local emergency management office and learn basic survival skills. Form a neighborhood group — for examples and suggestions, see Map Your Neighborhood at emd.wa.gov/myn.

## Actions to Take During an Earthquake — During the Shaking

Drop, Cover, and Hold On. Drop to your hands and knees before the earthquake knocks you down. Take cover under a sturdy table or desk and hold on to it firmly. If there is no table around, get lower than the other furniture — think BENEATH, BESIDE, or BETWEEN sturdy objects. Cover your head and neck with your arms. Stay put until the shaking stops.

## Actions to Take After an Earthquake — After the Shaking

Check your building for structural damage. Check yourself and others for injuries and apply basic first aid. Leave the building if it is unsafe. Shut off the water at the main valve. Help your neighbors, especially anyone with mobility limitations.

Only shut off gas if you smell natural gas, hear hissing, or see the dial spinning rapidly — otherwise leave the gas on, as gas service is difficult to restore. Tune in to local emergency radio stations, NOAA weather radio channels, your local television, or follow your local emergency management office on social media (Twitter @waEMD, facebook.com/WashEMD) for further instructions.
